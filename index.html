    Abstract
    
            
    This dashboard implements a multi-factor jump-diffusion forecasting layer over AI accelerator spot pricing across the public cloud and broker markets. We extend the classical Merton jump-diffusion (Merton, 1976) with a utilization-driven mean-reversion term and an explicit Poisson architecture-step jump intensity, motivated by the empirical observation that NVIDIA-cycle launch events compress prior-gen spot prices by 15–25% over ~90 days (Patel, 2024). Pure geometric Brownian motion is inadequate because secular Moore's-law drift and discrete vendor revaluations dominate idiosyncratic noise; structural agent-based models are over-parameterized at the available data window. The token-economics layer extends the price forecast to inference-workload cost-per-million-tokens at workload-specific FLOPS-utilization (Hoffmann et al., 2022).
    

            
    Stochastic model
    
            
    The AI accelerator spot market is structurally different from a commodities market because the underlying asset is on a Moore's-law curve and undergoes discrete architectural revaluations at vendor product launches. Naive geometric Brownian motion is therefore inappropriate: the secular price trajectory is downward (with intermittent jumps that abruptly accelerate the trajectory), the demand side reacts asymmetrically to large LLM training campaigns, and the supply side responds to hyperscaler capex on a quarterly cadence with multi-quarter lag. We model the log spot price p_t as a multi-factor jump-diffusion:
    
      
    d log p_t = ( α_t − β · u_t ) dt + σ dW_t + J_t dN_t
    
      
    The secular drift α_t is negative (~−15% to −30%/yr depending on SKU position relative to the architecture frontier). The utilization-driven mean-reversion term β · u_t, with u_t ∈ [0,1] measuring fractional capacity in use across the public-cloud pool, prevents the spot price from collapsing during low-demand periods: when u_t rises toward 1 the effective drift becomes more negative (scarcity rent compresses), which is counter-intuitive but matches the observed Q3 2023 H100 stickiness.
    
      
    The Poisson jump term J_t dN_t captures architecture-step events. Empirically (NVIDIA Hopper → Blackwell, Q4 2024) the launch of a next-gen SKU compresses the prior-gen spot by 15–25% over the subsequent 90-day window, with jump intensity λ ≈ 0.5/yr at NVIDIA's release cadence and lower for AMD/Google/AWS. J_t is drawn from a log-normal distribution with negative mean and variance estimated from the H100→B200, A100→H100, and V100→A100 transitions.
    
      
    The token-economics layer (extra2 panel) converts spot price to delivered-token cost via the throughput identity tokens/$ = (TFLOPS · η_util · ρ_workload) / ($/hr / 3600) where η_util is realized utilization on the workload and ρ_workload is the bytes-per-FLOP intensity (Llama-70B inference: ~1.8; Stable Diffusion XL inference: ~3.2; training: ~0.3).
    

            
    Calibration
    
            
    The secular drift α_t per SKU is calibrated against the public SemiAnalysis ASP curve and NVIDIA gross-margin disclosures in 10-Q filings. For NVIDIA SKUs the implied gross margin (>70% for Hopper, ≈68% for Blackwell pre-yield-mature) is consistent with the observed slow price decay; for AMD/Google/AWS the implied margins are lower (50–60%) and consistent with their faster initial price decay.
    
      
    Diffusion σ is estimated from the broker-quoted spot tape aggregated across CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, Crusoe, RunPod, Vast.ai, and TensorDock for the 2023-09 to 2025-12 window. Cross-broker spreads of 20–35% are normal due to capacity-allocation heterogeneity; we use the median spot per SKU per day.
    
      
    Utilization u_t is proxied by the spot-vs-reserved price ratio in the AWS p4d/p5/p5e instance families and by SemiAnalysis cluster-utilization estimates. The supply elasticity β is calibrated from the Q1 2024 H100 squeeze (utilization to 0.95, spot at $4.50/hr) and subsequent Q4 2024 normalization.
    
      
    Hyperscaler capex (extra panel) pulls the most recent guidance from earnings calls: Microsoft FY26 ~$88B, Google ~$75B, Meta ~$65B, Amazon ~

  
    05B (AWS only), Oracle ~$25B, ByteDance ~$20B. The 2026 total of ~$378B drives α_t through the supply-additions channel with a 2-quarter lag.
    

            
    Numerical implementation
    
            
    The MC engine generates 1000–10000 price paths via Euler–Maruyama with Poisson jumps for architecture-step events. The exact-step discretization is not applicable due to the jump component, so we use Δt = 1/252 (daily) which keeps the Euler bias under 0.5% for typical σ.
    
      
    The FLOPS/$ trajectory (extra2) applies a smooth Moore's-law factor of 1.4× per 24 months stacked with discrete +20–40% step revaluations at architecture launches. The 36-month curves shown in the second visualization panel use seed-deterministic noise so re-renders are stable.
    
      
    The tornado sensitivity panel (extra3) computes single-parameter elasticities ∂ log(spot) / ∂ θ_i at the calibrated baseline. The parameter grid heatmap (extra4) sweeps the (σ, jump-intensity) plane.
    

            
    Glossary & notation
    
            
    Symbols and domain terms used throughout the model. Symbol units are given where applicable.
    
            
    p_t
    Hourly spot price for a given SKU at time t. Units: USD/hr
    α_t
    Secular drift driven by Moore's-law and capex additions. Units: 1/yr (log)
    β
    Supply elasticity coefficient on utilization. Units: 1/yr (log)
    u_t
    Cross-broker utilization fraction. Units: fraction in [0,1]
    σ
    Idiosyncratic spot diffusion volatility. Units: 1/sqrt(yr)
    J_t
    Architecture-step jump magnitude. Units: log-fraction
    λ
    Jump intensity (Poisson rate). Units: 1/yr
    η_util
    Realized utilization on the workload (often 0.5–0.8). Units: fraction
    ρ_workload
    Workload intensity (bytes-per-FLOP). Units: B/FLOP
    TFLOPS
    Trillion floating-point ops per second (FP16 unless noted). Units: 10¹² ops/s
    PFLOPS
    Peta-FLOPS = 10³ TFLOPS. Units: 10¹⁵ ops/s
    Spot vs Reserved
    Ratio of on-demand spot to 1-yr reserved; high values signal scarcity. Units: ratio
    ASP
    Average Selling Price disclosed in vendor 10-K. Units: USD
    Capex
    Capital expenditure on AI infrastructure (hyperscaler annual guidance). Units: USD billions/yr
    

            
    Worked example
    
            
    End-to-end numerical walk-through of one Monte Carlo path for a representative SKU, showing how a single random draw translates through the model to a published statistic on the dashboard.
    
            
    1. Inputs. Select H100. Calibrated baseline: spot $2.49/hr, α = −0.18/yr, β = 0.05/yr, σ = 0.45/sqrt(yr), u_t = 0.62, jump intensity λ = 0.5/yr. 
    2. Drift contribution. Daily drift ≈ (α − β u) · Δt = (−0.18 − 0.05·0.62) · (1/252) = −0.000835. Without jumps, the 90-day expected log-return is −7.5%. 
      E[Δlog p | no jump]_{90d} = 90/252 · (−0.18 − 0.05 · 0.62) ≈ −0.075
    3. Jump risk. Probability of at least one architecture-step jump over 90 days: 1 − e^{−λ · 90/252} ≈ 16.4%. Conditional jump magnitude E[J] ≈ −18%. 
    4. Combined Monte Carlo. Run 5000 paths via Euler step. Empirical mean 90-day log-return: −10.1% (drift −7.5% + jump-risk-adjusted −2.6%). Empirical p05 = −24%, p95 = +6%. 
    5. Token-economics translation. At spot $2.49/hr and Llama-70B inference throughput 12.5 tokens/s, tokens-per-dollar ≈ 18,000. A 10% spot compression raises this to ~20,000. 
    

            
    Sensitivity analysis
    
            
    Tornado chart (extra3) shows that the architecture-step jump intensity λ dominates the 6-month forward variance — moving λ from 0.5/yr to 1.0/yr widens the 95% CI by 42%. Diffusion σ is second-largest; secular drift α matters most for the median but little for the tail. The parameter grid (extra4) shows that under (high σ, high λ) the realized tokens-per-dollar improvement over 12 months can exceed 3×.
    

            
    Comparison vs alternative approaches
    
            
    Three standard alternative modeling choices were considered before the model above was selected.
    
            
    • Pure GBM (no jumps). Cannot reproduce the abrupt H100 → B200 spot compression observed in Q1 2025; would underestimate forward variance by ~40%.
    • Hidden-Markov regime switching. Adds a hidden state for 'glut' vs 'squeeze' regimes; more flexible but the regime parameters are weakly identified at the 2-year window we have.
    • Structural agent-based model. Models individual hyperscalers as agents with budget constraints and capacity decisions; principled but requires assumptions about strategic behavior that exceed available data.
    

            
    Known limitations
    
            
    • Hyperscaler capex figures are point-in-time guidance and change quarterly; the dashboard does not auto-fetch updated guidance.
    • Tokens-per-dollar is workload-dependent: Llama-70B inference numbers will not transfer to GPT-class autoregressive or diffusion workloads. Multiply by 0.3 for training, 1.8 for diffusion.
    • ROCm vs CUDA tooling-gap penalties for AMD parts are not modeled — gross perf-per-$ overstates AMD's deployable advantage by an estimated 1.5× for typical fine-tuning workloads.
    • Energy costs ($0.06–0.12/kWh) are folded into spot rate, not modeled separately; the data-center PUE distinction (1.15 for Google vs ~1.4 for older AWS regions) is invisible to this model.
    • Geopolitical export-control shocks (e.g. October 2023 H100 China ban) are modeled only as jumps; no separate factor.
